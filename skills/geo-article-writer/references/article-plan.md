# Article Plan Reference

Use this reference to turn a keyword or topic into a GEO-friendly article plan.

## Plan Inputs

Extract or infer:

- Keyword or topic
- Business type or product context
- Search intent
- Why the topic matters
- Target reader
- Desired language
- Desired stance or core claim, if supplied
- Evidence supplied by the user

If the user gives only a keyword, infer a practical B2B or founder-blog angle and say the assumption briefly.

## Article Plan Schema

Build this internally before drafting:

```text
title
coreClaim
seoBlogHookPattern
canonicalHookPattern
hookPatternReason
openingMove
articlePromise
sections[]
keywordPlacement
writingNotes[]
```

Each section should include:

- heading
- purpose
- 2-3 concrete key points

Keyword placement should include:

- intro: how to introduce the exact keyword or close variant
- headers: 2-3 places where the keyword or close variant can appear
- body: how to use it naturally without stuffing

## Core Claim

Before drafting, define one core claim for every substantial article.

The core claim is the article's central argument. It should guide the title, opening, section choices, examples, and conclusion.

Strong core claims are usually:

- debatable rather than obvious
- specific to the reader's situation
- useful for decision-making
- inspectable through examples, evidence, or operational behavior
- narrow enough to make the article memorable

Weak:

- "AI search optimization is important."
- "Companies should create better content."
- "Good SEO requires quality."

Stronger:

- "Most B2B SaaS content is invisible to AI search because it describes features, not recommendation contexts."
- "For GEO, the first content gap is usually not keyword coverage; it is unclear entity and use-case definition."
- "The best AI-search articles behave less like thought leadership and more like structured decision support."

For neutral formats such as product documentation, release notes, compliance pages, or short definitions, soften the claim into a practical operating premise instead of forcing a contrarian argument.

## SEO Blog Hook Patterns

Choose exactly one.

### Research Findings Lead

Use when the article should start from evidence, analysis, observed patterns, benchmark data, or a practical finding.

Opening move:

- Lead with the finding or analysis scope.
- Move quickly to the business takeaway.

Best for:

- original research
- benchmark articles
- evidence-led explainers
- practical analysis

Canonical hook mapping:

- Proof-Led Claim
- Authority Through Specificity

### Misconception Correction Lead

Use when the article corrects a common belief, shallow tactic, or confused market narrative.

Opening move:

- State the mistaken assumption plainly.
- Replace it with a clearer operating frame.

Best for:

- myth-busting
- contrarian explainers
- "what teams get wrong" articles
- definition articles

Canonical hook mapping:

- Contrarian Data Correction
- Authority Through Specificity

### Timely Impact Lead

Use when the article is tied to a fresh platform change, market shift, search behavior shift, or new operational risk.

Opening move:

- Name what changed.
- Explain who it affects and what they should do next.

Best for:

- news reaction
- trend interpretation
- change management
- "why this matters now" pieces

Canonical hook mapping:

- Timely Shift
- Historical Parallel

### Framework Comparison Lead

Use when the article helps readers make a decision, compare options, classify situations, or choose a framework.

Opening move:

- Define the decision or categories.
- Explain the criteria and tradeoffs.

Best for:

- comparisons
- decision guides
- playbooks
- implementation articles

Canonical hook mapping:

- Reader Categorization
- Authority Through Specificity

## GEO Article Structure

A strong GEO article should include several of these:

- a visible core claim near the top
- a direct answer near the top
- clear definition of the core term
- structured sections with descriptive headings
- comparison or tradeoff table when useful
- concrete examples
- implementation checklist
- FAQ or answer blocks when the topic benefits from them
- citation-worthy claims supported by supplied evidence or marked for sourcing

## Title Rules

- Serious and article-first.
- Usually 8-18 words.
- It may paraphrase the keyword if the result is stronger.
- Avoid social-post style titles.
- Avoid clickbait and hype.

## Section Rules

Use 4-5 sections by default:

1. Explain the problem or shift.
2. State or unpack the core claim.
3. Define the practical framework.
4. Show how to apply it with a concrete artifact.
5. Address tradeoffs or mistakes, then end with a concise operating takeaway.

## Drafting Handoff

After planning, write the article from the plan.

Do not expose the full planning schema unless the user asks for process detail. The final answer may include a short "Article plan" summary after the article.
